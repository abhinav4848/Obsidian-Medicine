# Inguinal hernia
No benefit in identifying direct vs indirect.
Low risk of strangulation
Symptoms worse after exertion. 
Mostly male patients. 
#### Surgical management:
- Treat medically fit patients even if they are asymptomatic. 
- mesh repair has lowest recurrence rate
	- unilateral inguinal hernias are generally repaired with an open approach
	- bilateral and recurrent inguinal hernias are generally repaired laparoscopically
#### Return to Work
Open repair: 2-3 weeks
Laparoscopic: 1-2 weeks

# Femoral hernia
Femoral = Female, Fear (urgent referral)  
- high risk of strangulation 
- below and lateral to pubic tubercle
- difficult to reduce

Inguinal is Male, 
- Safety net (routine referral)
- Above and medial to pubic tubercle (i.e. closer to it pubic tubercle (medial))
# Other hernias
#### Richter Hernia
- Only part of the circumference of the bowel wall (anti-mesenteric border of the bowel) becomes trapped within a hernial sac, leading to bowel ischemia without causing a complete obstruction.
- Can present with strangulation without symptoms of obstruction
- Can occur at various sites, including inguinal or femoral regions. Highest risk of Richter hernia is in femoral hernia > obturator hernia
- They don't have specific clinical features that would make them more likely than other types of hernias
#### Spigelian hernia
- Result from a defect in the Spigelian fascia, which lies along the semilunar line (aka linea semilunaris: a vertical line running from just lateral to rectus abdominis muscle). 
- These hernias typically present with pain at or near their site
- They may not always be associated with an obvious palpable mass.
#### Obturator hernia
- Rare and occurs when abdominal contents protrude through the obturator canal, which is located in the pelvic sidewall. 
- Typically presents with medial thigh pain due to compression of the obturator nerve
#### Umbilical hernia
- Symmetrical bulge under the umbilicus
#### Paraumbilical hernia	
- Asymmetrical bulge - half the sac is covered by skin of the abdomen directly above or below the umbilicus
#### Epigastric hernia
- Lump in the midline between umbilicus and the xiphisternum.
- Risk factors include extensive physical training or coughing (from lung diseases), obesity
#### Incisional hernia
- May occur in up to 10% of abdominal operations
