Bias describes the situation in a trial where one outcome is systematically favoured. It should be noted that there is considerable variation in the definitions and classification of bias. The table below lists some of the more common types of bias.  

| **Type**                                | **Description**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Selection bias**                      | Error in assigning individuals to groups leading to differences which may influence the outcome. <br>- Subtypes include **sampling bias** where the subjects are not representative of the population. <br>- This may be due to **volunteer bias**. An example of volunteer bias would be a study looking at the prevalence of _Chlamydia_ in the student population. Students who are at risk of _Chlamydia_ may be more, or less, likely to participate in the study. A similar concept is **non-responder bias**. If a survey on dietary habits was sent out in the post to random households it is likely that the people who didn't respond would have poorer diets than those who did.  <br>  <br>Other examples include  <br><br>- loss to follow up bias<br>- prevalence/incidence bias (Neyman bias): when a study is investigating a condition that is characterised by early fatalities or silent cases. It results from missed cases being omitted from calculations<br>- admission bias (**Berkson's bias**): cases and controls in a hospital case control study are systematically different from one another because the combination of exposure to risk and occurrence of disease increases the likelihood of being admitted to the hospital<br>- healthy worker effect |
| **Procedure bias / Performance Bias**   | called performance Bias in RCTs. Researcher driven Bias.<br><br>Occurs when subjects in different groups receive different treatment. Differences arise because participants are treated differently, not just because of the intervention. Participants **change behaviour because they are being observed or engaged**. Related to Hawthorne effect. <br><br>e.g. <br>- **Intervention group gets**: Monthly weight checks, Dietary supplements, Motivational prompts<br>- **Control group gets**: Almost nothing (just final measurement)<br><br>This can: <br>- Change participants’ behaviour (they feel observed, motivated, supported)<br>- Improve outcomes independent of the actual intervention<br><br>More attention = better results (even if treatment doesn’t work)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| **Responder Bias**                      | Participants **change their behaviour because they are being studied** (Participant driven Bias)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| **Hawthorne effect**                    | A special form of responder Bias. Describes a group changing its behaviour due to the knowledge that it is being studied, not due to the actual intervention.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| **Recall bias**                         | Difference in the accuracy of the recollections retrieved by study participants, possibly due to whether they have disorder or not. E.g. a patient with lung cancer may search their memories more thoroughly for a history of asbestos exposure than someone in the control group. A particular problem in case-control studies.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| **Publication / Reporting bias**        | Failure to publish results from valid studies, often as they showed a negative or uninteresting result. Important in meta-analyses where studies showing negative results may be excluded.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               |
| **Work-up bias (verification bias)**    | In studies which compare new diagnostic tests with gold standard tests, work-up bias can be an issue. Sometimes clinicians may be reluctant to order the gold standard test unless the new test is positive, as the gold standard test may be invasive (e.g. tissue biopsy). <br><br>This approach can seriously distort the results of a study, and alter values such as specificity and sensitivity. Sometimes work-up bias cannot be avoided, in these cases it must be adjusted for by the researchers.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| **Expectation bias (Pygmalion effect)** | Only a problem in non-blinded trials. Observers may subconsciously measure or report data in a way that favours the expected study outcome.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| **Late-look bias**                      | Gathering information at an inappropriate time e.g. studying a fatal disease many years later when some of the patients may have died already<br><br>By reviewing the patient's notes after she has already been diagnosed with Hodgkin lymphoma, you are examining the data with knowledge of the outcome. This creates a significant risk that you will interpret earlier, possibly subtle signs and symptoms as being obvious indicators of lymphoma, when they may have been appropriately assessed as non-specific at the time. For instance, you might notice entries about mild fatigue or occasional night sweats from months earlier and conclude these were missed early signs, when in reality they may have been too vague to warrant investigation at that time. This retrospective analysis with the benefit of hindsight distorts the true clinical picture and decision-making process that occurred in real-time. In research terms, this approach would systematically bias your findings toward identifying 'missed opportunities' that weren't actually clinically apparent at the time.                                                                                                                                                                             |
| **Lead-time bias**                      | Occurs when two tests for a disease are compared, the new test diagnoses the disease earlier, but there is no effect on the outcome of the disease. <br><br>Or, situation where survival will appear to be better in cases detected by screening, without there necessarily being a real difference between the survival of screened and unscreened populations.<br><br>When evaluating the effectiveness of the early detection and treatment of a condition, the lead time must be subtracted from the overall survival time of screened patients to avoid lead time bias. Historically, this has been a flaw in the evaluation of cancer screening programmes and, in particular, breast cancer screening programmes, resulting in inflated claims of effectiveness.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
| **Ceiling effect**                      | Occurs when an independent variable ceases to have an effect on a measured dependent variable because a 'ceiling' or maximum effect has been attained. An example may be participants scoring a maximum score in a test or game (there is no option to measure higher and therefore the ceiling is reached), or in pharmacology, where the effect of a medication plateaus at a saturation point.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| **Observer bias**                       | occurs when the observer or researcher records information that differs from the true occurrence due to seeing what they want or expect to see rather than what they actually see.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| Attrition Bias / Loss to follow up bias | Occurs when:<br>- Participants drop out<br>- Dropout differs between groups<br><br>**Example**: More people leave the intervention group → results skewed                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
