Miscarriage occurs in approximately 25% of all recognised pregnancies. The majority of sporadic losses before 10 weeks' gestation result from random numeric chromosomal errors, specifically: [[childhood syndromes|trisomy]] (Down, Edward, Patau), monosomy and polyploidy. Women over the age of 35 years have a markedly increased risk of trisomy pregnancies. 

For women with a miscarriage diagnosed before 13 weeks' gestation, expectant management for 7-14 days is the first-line option, in the absence of factors such as evidence of infection, or increased risk of bleeding.

# Recurrent Miscarriage
\>= 3 miscarriages in first trimester (doesn't have to be consecutive or with same partner). 
- Lupus anticoagulant and anticardiolipin antibodies
- Pelvic ultrasound to assess for uterine anomalies
- Thyroid function tests and thyroid peroxidase (TPO) antibodies

Factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation and protein S deficiency may be considered for any second trimester miscarriage. 


Clinicians are encouraged to use their clinical discretion to recommend extensive evaluation after two first trimester miscarriages, if there is a suspicion that the miscarriages are of pathological and not of sporadic nature. - *green top guideline 17*
