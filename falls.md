For older patients at risk of falling, multifactorial intervention programmes that involve 
- strength and balance training, 
- home hazard assessment and intervention, 
- vision assessment and referral, and 
- medication review are recommended. 
Brisk walking, cognitive behavioural therapy, hip protectors and vitamin D all have insufficient evidence to demonstrate any benefit.

# Falls in the elderly
As individuals age, they become more likely to fall. Falls can occur due to a range of factors and are associated with significant mortality and morbidity in the elderly population. Around one-third of elderly people living in the community fall every year. Not only do falls often lead to injuries they also impact patients confidence and independence. Therefore it is important to understand the causes of falls and how they may be prevented.  
  
**Gait and falling**  
Normal gait involves:  
- The neurological system - basal ganglia and cortical basal ganglia loop.
- The musculoskeletal system (which must have appropriate tone and strength).
- Effective processing of the senses such as sight, sound, and sensation (fine touch and proprioception).  
As individuals get older they are more likely to experience medical problems affecting these systems, which leads to gait abnormalities and increases the risk of falls.  
## Risk factors for falling
Falls often occur due to a range of factors and individuals who have fallen previously are at a significantly higher risk of falling again. Other risk factors include:  
- Lower limb muscle weakness
- Vision problems
- Balance/gait disturbances (diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis and parkinson's disease etc)
- Polypharmacy (4+ medications)
- Incontinence
- >65
- Have a fear of falling
- Depression
- Postural hypotension
- Arthritis in lower limbs
- Psychoactive drugs
- Cognitive impairment

Some studies have shown that individuals with 4 or more risk factors have up to a 78% chance of falling It is not possible to remove all risk factors but it is important to limit these where possible.  
**Risk assessment**  
Ideally, all patients should be screened for falls risk in order to establish the level of support they need, particularly when they are in a hospital or a home.  
  
It is important to establish the following from the history:  
- Where was the patient when they fell?
- When did they fall?
- Did anyone else see the patient fall? (collateral history)
- What happened? Were there any associated features before/during/after
- Why do they think they fell?
- Have they fallen before?
- Systems review
- Past medical history (especially issues related to balance/sight/gait)
- Social history
## Medication and medication reviews
Reviewing a patients medication is crucial to reduce the chances of falling again. Patients on more than four drugs are more likely to fall therefore any suspect or unnecessary medications should be stopped or swapped. 

|Medications that cause postural hypotension|Medications associated with falls due to other mechanisms|
|---|---|
|Nitrates|Benzodiazepines|
|Diuretics|Antipsychotics|
|Anticholinergic medications|Opiates|
|Antidepressants|Anticonvulsants|
|Beta-blockers|Codeine|
|L-Dopa|Digoxin|
|Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors - (ACE) inhibitors|Other sedative agents|
## Examining the patient with a fall
It is essential that patients who have fallen are assessed thoroughly. Therefore a full A to E approach and assessment of all systems is important to rule out the cause.  
## Investigation of falls

| Investigations to consider |                                                                                |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Bedside tests              | Basic observations, blood pressure, blood glucose, urine dip and ECG           |
| Bloods                     | Full Blood Count, Urea and Electrolytes, Liver function tests and bone profile |
| Imaging                    | X-ray of chest/injured limbs, CT head and cardiac echo                         |
**Management**  
- Identify all individuals who have fallen in the last 12 months.
- As per above identify why they are at risk.
- For those with a falls history or at risk complete the 'Timed Up and Go test'
### Timed Up and Go test
How to perform  
- The patient starts seated in a standard chair.
- On the clinician’s command (“Go”):
    - The patient stands up from the chair.
    - Walks 3 metres (10 feet) at a comfortable pace.
    - Turns around, walks back, and sits down again.
- The clinician observes how steady and safe the movements are.
- In the Timed Up and Go (TUG) version, the total time is recorded (from standing up to sitting down again).

| Time take     | Interpretation                                                  |
| ------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| <10 seconds   | Normal mobility                                                 |
| 10–20 seconds | Fair – mostly independent but may have mild gait/balance issues |
| >20 seconds   | Impaired mobility – increased risk of falls                     |
| >30 seconds   | Suggests high fall risk and likely functional dependence        |
Offer a multidisciplinary assessment by a qualified clinician to all patients over 65 with:  
- >2 falls in the last 12 months
- A fall that requires medical treatment
- Poor performance or failure to complete the 'Turn 180° test' or the 'Timed up and Go test

Individuals who fall but do not meet these criteria should be reviewed annually and given written information on falls.
