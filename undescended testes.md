Occurs in around 2-3% of term male infants, but is much more common if the baby is preterm. Around 25% of cases are bilateral.

*From the [[newborn Infant physical examination|NIPE]] guide.* 
## Complications of undescended testis 
- infertility
- torsion
- [[cancer- testes|testicular cancer]] (Germ cell tumour e.g. seminomas)
- psychological

# Assessment
Identify undescended testes within 72 hrs of birth. Further identify them by 6-8 weeks age at infant screening.

**Screen positive criteria**
- b/l impalpable testes
- u/l impalpable testes +/- hypospadias
- u/l palpable testis not located in scrotum, + hypospadias
- disorders of sexual development (prev called ambiguous genitalia)

# Management
- They get reviewed < 24 hrs after birth, by senior paediatricians if b/l undescended
- All babies with one or both testis/testes that are palpable, but not located in the scrotum should have a primary care (GP) review between 4 and 5 months of age and referral to surgeon if testis/ testes are still undescended or impalpable (to be seen no later than 6 months of age).
- Spontaneous descent beyond this is unlikely and undescended or impalpable testes are best managed by surgical intervention

### One/Both testes palpable but not in scrotum
Review at birth -> if palpable but not in scrotum -> review at infant screening. If still not descended, then again at 4-5 months (corrected for gestational age). If inguinal testes felt between inguinal canal and scrotum, it may be gently manipulated into the scrotum. 
- Referral should be considered from around 3 months of age, with the baby ideally seeing a urological / paeds surgeon not later than 6 months of age

### Bilateral Impalpable undescended testes
May be associated with underlying endocrine disorders (such as [[congenital adrenal hyperplasia]]) and ambiguous genitalia and therefore early paediatric input is necessary. 
- Should be reviewed by a senior paediatrician within 48 hours as the child may need urgent endocrine or genetic investigation. 
- If no sexual disorder suspected, review at 6-8 weeks, then 4-5 months.
- If still not descended -> Refer to paeds surgery / urology. 

**If there are bilateral impalpable undescended testes at 6–8 weeks of age:**
- Arrange urgent referral for the infant to be seen by a senior paediatrician within 48 hours.
# Extra Reading
- Testes that are palpable at both screening examinations may ascend later in childhood, so any concerns expressed by parents should be addressed. 
- **Orchidopexy**: Surgical practices vary although the majority of procedures are performed at around 1 year of age.
