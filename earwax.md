Ear wax is a normal physiological substance which helps protect the ear canal. Impacted ear wax is extremely common and may cause a variety of symptoms including:  
- pain
- conductive hearing loss
- tinnitus
- vertigo

The main treatment options in primary care are ear drops or irrigation ('ear syringing'). The following drops may be used 3–4 times daily for 3–5 days initially. 
- olive oil
- sodium bicarbonate 5%
- almond oil (not if allergic to them)

Options: 
- Ear irrigation (ear syringing. flushing the wax out using water) using an electronic irrigator.
	- Don't offer manual syringing as water pressure can't be controlled. 
- Microsuction (using a vacuum to suck the wax out under a microscope), or 
- another method of earwax removal (such as manual removal using a probe) may be considered

Irrigation Contraindications ([Rotherham earwax](https://www.earcarecentre.com/uploadedFiles/Pages/Health_Professionals/Protocols/Ear%20irrigation%20guidelines%202022.pdf))
- Grommets  
- Otitis media within the last 6 weeks
- If a perforation, active dermatitis, is suspected
- There is evidence of active infection of EAC: Acute otitis externa with pain and tenderness of the pinna 
- The patient has previously experienced complications following this procedure in the past 
- ANY form of ear surgery (apart from grommets that have extruded at least 18 months previously and it is documented subsequently that the tympanic membrane is intact)
- The patient has a healed perforation
- There is a history of a mucous discharge in the past twelve months 
- there is a history of cleft palate, repaired or not
- The patient has abnormalities of the meatus such as exostosis
- The patient has hearing in only one ear if it is the ear to be treated
