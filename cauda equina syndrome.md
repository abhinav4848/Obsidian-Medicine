# Red flags for MRI based on Leicester A&E guidelines
- Nerve
	- Sense of numbness or pins & needles around anus, genitals or inner thighs
	- Bilateral radicular (below knee) leg pain of sudden onset, or sciatica that has progressed from unilateral to bilateral
	- Severe or progressive bilateral leg weakness affecting knee extension, ankle eversion or foot dorsiflexion
- Faecal
	- Loss of sensation of rectal fullness
	- Faecal incontinence
	- Decreased anal tone
		- It is good practice to check anal tone in patients with new-onset back pain. However, studies show this has poor sensitivity and specificity for CES
- Urinary dysfunction
	- Difficulty initiating micturition
	- Impaired sensation of urinary flow
	- Painless urinary retention
	- Urinary incontinence _(unless features suggest stress or urge incontinence)_
		- incontinence is a late sign that may indicate irreversible damage
- Inability to achieve erection or ejaculate
- Loss of genital sensation

Bilateral radicular leg pain only (NO other red flags as per the list above)
AND normal neurology 
AND PVR ≤200 mL
-> Discharge, as 'At risk of CES', so will need 2ww spinal review. 

Safety net: [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FdlxfcJmn-4) - Vetted by Leicester A&E & included on the pathway for any low back pain. 
# Investigation 
- urgent MRI
# Management  
- surgical decompression
