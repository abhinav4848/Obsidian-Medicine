Very common in children.

# Clinical Features
Infestation often presents with intense perianal itching, which is typically worse during the night.
- Some people may be asymptomatic and only become aware of infection when small white thread-like worms (which may be slowly moving) are seen on the perianal skin or in the stools.
- In females, the genital area can also be involved -> pruritus vulvae.
- Nocturnal itching may lead to disturbed sleep and irritability.

# Investigation
Diagnosis may be made by the applying Sellotape to the perianal area and sending it to the laboratory for microscopy to see the eggs. However, most patients are treated empirically and this approach is supported in the CKS guidelines.

# Treatment
All household contacts should be treated, even if asymptomatic. 
Single dose of mebendazole should be taken by all family members on the same day. Between 6m to 2yo, mebendazole is unlicensed. 

exception: 
- pregnant/breastfeeding, children <2yo.
- Strict hygiene measures alone are advised to disrupt the life cycle of the worms (eggs are laid on the perianal skin but only hatch if ingested e.g. if patient scratches then eats without washing hands).
