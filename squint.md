Squint (strabismus) is characterised by misalignment of the visual axes. 
It is important to detect as uncorrected may lead to ==amblyopia== (the brain fails to fully process inputs from one eye and over time favours the other eye).
# Types
Squints may be divided into concomitant (common) and paralytic (rare). 

| **Concomitant**                                                                       | **Paralytic**                           |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Due to imbalance in extraocular muscles  <br>Convergent is more common than divergent | Due to paralysis of extraocular muscles |
## Classification
Squints may be classified as to where the eye deviates toward (Affected eye moves from starting position to normal position when the other eye is occluded.)
- the nose: esotropia
- temporally: exotropia
- superiorly: hypertropia
- inferiorly: hypotropia
# Test
Corneal light reflection test - holding a light source 30cm from the child's face to see if the light reflects symmetrically on the pupils  
  
The cover test is used to identify the nature of the squint  
- ask the child to focus on an object
- cover one eye
- observe movement of uncovered eye
- cover other eye and repeat test
# Management 
Referral to secondary care
Eye patches may help prevent amblyopia

> [!Question] Question
> A mother brings her son in to surgery as she suspects he has a squint. She thinks his right eye is 'turned inwards'. You perform a cover test to gather further information. Which one of the following findings would be consistent with a right esotropia?
> 
> **Answer**: On covering the left eye the right eye moves laterally to take up fixation (i.e. points at the target)
