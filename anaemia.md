# Anaemia in pregnancy
Pregnant women are screened for anaemia at:  
- the booking visit (often done at 8-10 weeks), and at
- 28 weeks

NICE use the following cut-offs to determine whether a woman should receive oral iron therapy:  

|Gestation|Cut-off|
|---|---|
|First trimester|< 110 g/L|
|Second/third trimester|< 105 g/L|
|Postpartum|< 100 g/L|
### Management  
Oral Ferrous sulphate or ferrous fumarate (100-200mg elemental iron)
- treatment should be continued for 3 months after iron deficiency is corrected to allow iron stores to be replenished

| Factors inhibiting iron absorption                              | Factors that enhance iron absorption              |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Foods rich in calcium, <br>Tannins in tea<br>Phytates in cereal | Heme iron<br>Ferrous iron (Fe2+)<br>Ascorbic acid |
# Low Ferritin in children
If a child has low ferritin, prefer liquid formulation of 
- Ferrous fumarate [BNFc](https://bnfc.nice.org.uk/drugs/ferrous-fumarate/#indications-and-dose)
- Ferrous gluconate
- Sodium feredetate [BNFc](https://bnfc.nice.org.uk/drugs/sodium-feredetate/) (Often marketed as Sytron syrup. Historically common in paediatrics.)
If that doesn't suit the child, they can try iron gummies off the counter.
# Target Ferritin in CKD
Anaemia in CKD - aim for 10-12 g/dl (even after starting on Erythropoietin)

# Ferritin is an acute phase reactant
- Can be high in inflammation, infection, liver disease.
