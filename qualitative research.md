Qualitative research is a type of research that focuses on understanding experiences, meanings, and perspectives rather than measuring numerical data. It explores the **why** and **how** of a phenomenon, rather than just the what, where, or how many.  
  
### Key features:
- Data type: Non-numerical, often words, images, or observations (e.g. interview transcripts, diaries, focus group discussions).
- Purpose: To gain in-depth insight into people’s attitudes, beliefs, motivations, and behaviours.
- Methods: Common approaches include interviews, focus groups, participant observation, case studies, and document analysis.
- Analysis: Looks for themes, patterns, and meanings within the data, often using methods like thematic analysis, grounded theory, or discourse analysis.
- Output: Rich, descriptive accounts that help explain why things happen in a certain way, often highlighting context, complexity, and nuance.
# Approaches
- A qualitative ‘approach’ refers to the overarching framework that guides how the research is conducted, including its purpose, the researcher’s role, data collection, and analysis.

- Ethnography
    - Focuses on studying entire cultures or groups through **participant observation**.
    - The researcher immerses themselves in the environment, collecting detailed field notes without a fixed endpoint or predefined focus.
- Phenomenology
    - Aims to describe individuals’ **lived experience** of a particular phenomenon, often through in-depth interviews.
- Grounded Theory
    - Seeks to generate theory that is **grounded in observed data**.
    - Begins with open-ended questions; theoretical categories emerge over time.
    - Moves from initial exploration to verification, culminating in a central conceptual theme.
# Methods
- Commonly used qualitative methods include **interviews** and **focus groups**, particularly in healthcare settings.
# Sampling
- Convenience sampling
    - Participants are recruited on an open, first-come basis until target numbers are reached.
- Purposive sampling
    - Participants are selected based on characteristics relevant to the research question (e.g. HIV-positive women in an urban area).
- Quota sampling
    - A more specific form of purposive sampling.
    - Subgroups are selected to mirror proportions seen in the wider population.
- Snowball sampling
    - Initial participants refer others from their networks.
    - Commonly used for **hidden or hard-to-reach populations**.
- Case study sampling
    - Involves selecting a **single case** (e.g. person, organisation, or event) for in-depth study.
# Sample size (data saturation)
- Sample size is typically determined by **data saturation** – when further data no longer generates new categories, explanations, or insights.
# Assessing validity
- Triangulation
    - Uses **multiple data sources or methods** (e.g. interviews and observations) to enhance credibility.
- Respondent validation (member checking)
    - Researchers check that participants agree with or recognise the findings or interpretations.
- Bracketing:
    - In phenomenology, involves setting aside the researcher’s prior beliefs to reduce bias.
- Reflexivity:
    - A continuous process where researchers reflect on how their background, assumptions, and involvement shape the research.
# Analytical approaches
- Content analysis:
    - Transcribed data is categorised and analysed, often with coding software.
    - May include **counting word frequencies** and identifying recurring themes.
- Constant comparison:
    - Drawn from grounded theory.
    - Themes are refined continuously as new data is compared with previous data, ensuring a **systematic audit trail**
