# Adrenal insufficiency & Addison's Disease
NICE recommends that for those ≤16y, we follow the sick day rules from [British Society of Paediatric Endocrinology and Diabetes (BSPED) Consensus Guidelines on Adrenal Insufficiency](https://www.bsped.org.uk/adrenal-insufficiency). We refer you there because the sites includes calculated doses based on patient weight and surface area.

NICE covers the sick day rules for those ≥16y, and its recommendations are set out in the table below. We have included recommendations for inpatients, surgery and pregnancy, for completeness. The decisions relating to most of these groups of patients will not be taken in primary care.

Note that patients on modified-release hydrocortisone need a supply of immediate-release hydrocortisone to cover sick day situations such as those above.

## Sick days rules for significant PHYSIOLOGICAL stress 
NICE defines physiological stress as:
- Fever.
- Physical trauma requiring medical attention.
- Invasive procedure/surgery.
- Pregnancy/pregnancy loss/labour.

## Sick day ‘guidance’
This is the concept that patients on potentially nephron-harmful drugs stop them at times of intercurrent illness where fluid balance may be disturbed (e.g. gastroenteritis). The key thing here is that the patient is the one who decides when to stop them and when to restart them, which is a whole different thing from us seeing someone and telling them to stop a drug for a few days (e.g. statins with erythromycin). 

**The NHS ‘Think Kidneys’ campaign is concerned that overuse or strict interpretation of the guidance may result in harm,** and prefers the term ‘sick day guidance’ to ‘sick day rules’ or ‘drug holidays’. The ‘sick day’ advice is based on the consensus, not evidence ([Sick day guidance in patients at risk of AKI: a position statement from the Think Kidneys board, Jan 2018](https://www.thinkkidneys.nhs.uk/aki/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2018/01/Think-Kidneys-Sick-Day-Guidance-2018.pdf)). Its concerns are:

- The evidence is very weak that sick day guidance reduces harm.
- There are potentials for harms if sick day guidance is widely applied to people at low risk of AKI (in whom continuing the drugs may actually be better for them).
- Patients not being sure whether they are ill enough to need to heed the advice.
- People with heart failure decompensating when ACE inhibitors/diuretics are stopped.
- Hypertension or diabetic control being poor if people are repeatedly stopping medications because of illness.
- People may be put-off taking drugs, even when they are well, because they are ‘nephrotoxic’ (==try to avoid this term!==).
- Patients over-interpreting advice and stopping medication unnecessarily, such as during minor illness.
- Patients forgetting or choosing not to restart after an episode of ill health.
- If patients are doing this themselves at home, drugs that should be titrated up are not being.
- People not seeking help when they should because they think they can manage independently.
- Problems with achieving brief cessations of treatment in people using dosette boxes.
- It may also be theoretically possible that ACE inhibitors/ARBs might reduce the severity and duration of AKI!

## So, what sick day guidance is recommended? 
The current advice from the NHS ‘**Think Kidneys**’ campaign is that:
- We should talk to patients about the risks of AKI, including the need to maintain fluid intake at times of illness.
- Sick day guidance can be given to those at high risk of AKI, after an individual risk assessment.
- Systematic application of sick day guidance should only be undertaken in the context of a formal evaluation. 

## What constitutes a ‘sick day’?
The NHS ‘Think Kidneys’ campaign definition of ‘acute illness’:
 _When you are unwell with any of the following:_
- _Vomiting or diarrhoea (unless minor)._
- _Fevers, sweats and shaking._

## Which drugs are included in sick day guidance? %%linked%%
[London Kidney Network NHS](https://londonkidneynetwork.nhs.uk/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Sick-Day-Rules.pdf)
- [[nsaid|Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] (because they impair renal autoregulation).
- Drugs that lower BP or cause volume depletion (increased risk of AKI by reducing glomerular perfusion):
    - [[ace inhibitors|ACE inhibitors]] and ARBs.
    - Diuretics, including [[spironolactone]] and eplerenone.
    - Other blood pressure-lowering drugs.
- Drugs that might accumulate as a result of reduced kidney function in AKI, increasing the risks of adverse effects:
    - Metformin (increased risk of lactic acidosis).
    - [[diabetes drugs|Sulfonylureas]] (increased risk of hypoglycaemia, because drug cleared through kidneys).
    - [[Trimethoprim]] (increased risk of [[hyperkalemia|Hyperkalaemia]], and may also cause a ‘false’ rise in creatinine by interfering with tubular creatinine secretion, resulting in a ‘false positive’ diagnosis of AKI).
    - SGLT2 inhibitors, Direct renin inhibitors (aliskiren)

## So, what does this mean in practice?
The following may be a reasonable approach:

**In those at high risk of AKI, such as those who have had it before, those with comorbidities (especially heart failure, diabetes and CKD) or older/frailer people:**

- Make sure they understand the importance of maintaining a good fluid intake at times of acute illness.
- Explain the sick day guidance if you think they will be able to follow it, and you think the potential benefits outweigh the risks outlined above.
- Make sure they understand that this does not replace seeking clinical advice when unwell.
- Perhaps suggest they ring us each time they stop their drugs so we can decide if this is appropriate (this can stop if you are confident they are doing it correctly).

Widespread application of sick day guidance to everybody on the relevant drugs is probably not appropriate (so, have you talked to your local pharmacy about this?!).
