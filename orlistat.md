Also see: [[Obesity in children]]. 
Orlistat assists with weight loss, without affecting the brain or suppressing appetite. 
# MoA
Orlistat is a gastrointestinal lipase inhibitor- Reduces the absorption of fat from the gut.
It inhibits pancreatic lipase and prevents fat breakdown in the GI tract therefore reduces intestinal absorption of fat.
### Adverse effects:
Patients must stick to a very low-fat diet to avoid unpleasant side effects like: 
- faecal urgency/incontinence and 
- flatulence. 
A lower dose version is now available without prescription ('Alli').
# Prescribing criteria
It should only be prescribed as part of an overall plan for managing obesity in adults who have:  
- BMI >= 28 + associated risk factors, or
- BMI of 30 
- The patient should be reviewed 12 weeks after commencing orlistat & continued only if weight loss of at least 5% at 3 months. (as measured at the start of drug treatment).
- orlistat is normally used for < 1 year.

The new guidelines make an exception in [[diabetes treatment|type 2 diabetes]]: 
- Rates of weight loss may be slower in people with T2DM, so less strict goals (for example a loss of more than 3% of their body weight in 12 weeks) may be appropriate.
# Long term effects
> [!warning]
> Risk of fat soluble [[vitamin deficiency]]- A, D, E and K.

Consideration to all nutrients should be given ([[calcium]] would also be important to consider for bone health).
