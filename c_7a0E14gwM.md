Start with the problem people experience. 
Each time you explain a new topic, first go over what is commonly known or spoken about regarding that known topic. 

The problem is with how you structure your eating patterns throughout the day. 
HTN is driven by signal persistence, the hormones that are elevated throughout the day. The pressure system doesn't get a chance to stand down. 
Insulin. 
Insulin rise and fall is a signal to all the organs of the body about what state you're in at that time. 
- Kidneys: hold sodium. 
- Blood vessels/endothelial dysfunction: In a certain mode of reactivity. The endothelium doesn't get a chance to produce nitric oxide which allows the blood vessel to relax. 
- Sympathetic system to stay slightly more active. 
All of that is normal when you're eating. 

The problem is when insulin never falls. When it stays elevated for 12/14/16 hrs a day. 
Modern eating patterns have made it the default for most people. Frequent snacking or eating a meal means not enough break given to allow insulin to return to baseline, so you have chronically elevated insulin. 
Not high enough to be a problem on blood tests but high enough to change how your kidneys, blood vessels, and autonomic nervous system behave over a course of weeks/ months/ years. 

All these constant elevated states mean that your blood pressure is nudged upwards over a long period of time., even when you're eating less sodium, doing everything else right. 

## kidney
Journal: Hypertension. 
Insulin directly stimulates the epithelial sodium channels in the kidney's collecting duct leading to increased sodium absorption independent of dietary sodium intake. The increased demand by the body to digest food means more blood volume is needed to absorb the nutrients from the food. So, the kidneys hold onto the sodium to allow for this high demand state. This is fine for a short while during food reabsorption which then stops once you stop eating, insulin drops, and kidneys release the sodium and water out. 
But this permanent elevated state of insulin due to frequent snacking/meals causes this state to become permanent. Even morning hypertension can be due to late night snacking, be it an apple or a few nuts. 

## BMI
if someone has normal BMI, if exercising regularly and look fit, we assume their internal metabolic machinery is normal. This isn't true. 
We have found patients called TOFI- Thin outside, fat inside. 

With advanced imaging like CT/MRI, we have found people with high visceral fat, particularly around the liver. Visceral fat is metabolically active in a way that s.c. fat, the fat you can pinch, isn't. 

In Journal obesity research: 1-in-4 people had metabolic abnormalities typically assoc with obesity, like insulin resistance and HTN. 
Visceral fat releases inflammatory signals & interferes with insulin signalling in the liver. This leads to insulin resistance in the liver which among the primary drivers of chronically elevated insulin levels throughout the day. 
When you're insulin resistant, the pancreas have to produce more insulin to achieve the same level of blood sugar control. 

## Where is this more common
Women going through menopause lose muscle mass & gain visceral fat even if their overall weight stays same. Chronic sleep deprived people accumulate more visceral fat. Moderate alcohol intake which most people consider healthy also leads to fatty liver and insulin resistance. 
Chronic stress elevates cortisol which preferentially deposits fat in the liver.   

The clinical implications: You can't judge someone's BP risk just by looking at them. 
The culprit: Constant eating/Grazing. Liquid calories: Smoothie in morning, orange juice with lunch, protein shake after gym, couple glasses of wine at dinner. Each of them might seem harmless on their own. 
Liquid carbs and liquid proteins are absorbed more quickly, so they elevate insulin more quickly and leave a long tail of insulin after the drink has finished. 
Healthy snacks: If having them between meals, you prevent insulin from dropping.
Late night eating: Even if something small, insulin stays elevated for several hours after. It extends the insulin into the hours when the body is supposed to be in deep metabolic rest. 

You're preventing the body from entering the fasted state where a lot of the beneficial metabolic recalibrations happen- where insulin falls, GH rises, autophagy kicks in, and from HTN perspective, kidneys are given the signal to release the sodium. 

# Why the modern food environment works against you
Packaged as essential fuel for your busy day. 
Protein bars, the nutpacks, the dried fruit, the granola that you think are healthy. 

The path of least resistance leads to metabolic dysregulation.
# what actually lowers BP biologically
You need to create windows of time where insulin can fall to baseline. Not just lower than what it was an hour ago, but actually back to baseline. 
Condense your eating time. 
If you are eating from 7am to 11pm. Have your first meal at mid day instead of breakfast. 
cut out snack time and have dinner early. 

Exact structure doesn't matter. 

Journal: cell metabolism. Restricting eating to a 10 hour window without changing what people ate actually reduced their blood pressure, improvement in cholesterol profiles and better glucose regulation. 

# Movement also matters
Exercise benefit on BP isn't just from burning calories or improving cardiovascular fitness. Exercise also acutely improves insulin sensitivity. When you exercise, (particularly resistance training, mod-high intensity cardio), your muscles become more receptive to insulin for several hours afterwards. 

Journal applied physiology: A single bout of moderate exercise can improve insulin sensitivity for upto 48 hrs. That means you need less insulin to achieve the same level of blood sugar control so less overall insulin - less sodium retention. 

Timing your largest meal or your carb intake around this exercise takes advantage of this window & minimises the duration of insulin elevation throughout the rest of the day.  

## Sleep
This is when body performs most of its hormonal recalibration. GH rises, Cortisol drops to its lowest levels in the early parts of the night. Insulin sensitivity improves with sleep & deteriorates with sleep deprivation. Just 4 nights of sleep restriction reduced insulin sensitivity by approx 30% in healthy adults. 
Chronic undersleeping is an uphill battle against BP.
