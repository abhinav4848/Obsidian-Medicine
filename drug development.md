**Pilot study** is a small-scale preliminary study conducted to evaluate feasibility, time, cost, adverse events, and improve upon the study design prior to performance of a full-scale research project. Pilot studies are typically conducted before Phase 1 trials or between phases to refine protocols. They are not a formal phase of clinical drug development and would not be the type of study that compares a new antidepressant with existing treatments before approval.

# Phase 1
- small studies (e.g. 100) on healthy volunteers
- used to assess pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics, as well as establish safe dose ranges. 

# Phase 2  
- small studies (e.g. 100-300) on actual patients
- examines efficacy, adverse effects

# Phase 3  
- larger studies (e.g. 500-5,000 patients having the condition the drug is designed to treat)
- may compare drug with existing treatments (standard of care) to see if new drug offers any advantages in terms of efficacy, safety, convenience & also for adverse effects
- studies of special groups e.g. renal, elderly
If drug shown to be safe and effective then drugs may be approved for marketing
# Phase 4  
- post-marketing surveillance
